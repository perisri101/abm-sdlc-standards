Executive Summary

This document is the ABM Universal SDLC — the single mandatory standard governing how all ABM IT teams build, test, and release software. It applies to every development paradigm: full code, low-code, no-code, and SaaS/PaaS configuration. Teams extend it for their specific stack; they never reduce it.

What This Document Establishes
One common languageEvery team — from Team Connect to Boomi to Oracle — speaks the same SDLC vocabulary
Non-negotiable controlsSource control, access segregation, security scanning, environment separation, and CAB approval are universal
Extensible frameworkTeams add practices for their stack; child documents reference this master and augment — never subtract
Audit-ready by designEvery phase produces artifacts that directly satisfy ISO 27001 A.8 controls owned by Trilok Manchinila
Git-centric enforcementBranch protection, PR gates, and CI/CD pipelines enforce the standards — providing systematic, auditable enforcement that complements human review
ISO 27001 Alignment
This document serves as primary evidence for controls A.8.4, A.8.25, A.8.26, A.8.27, A.8.28, A.8.29, A.8.31, A.8.33, and A.5.23 — all assigned to the Executive Sponsor. Each section is tagged with the controls it satisfies.

Purpose & Scope

2.1 Purpose

To define the mandatory processes, controls, and practices governing software delivery across all of ABM IT — ensuring consistent quality, security, compliance, and audit readiness regardless of platform or paradigm.

2.2 Scope

Applies to all ABM IT development activity:

Full-Code (Python, .NET, Java, Node) Low-Code (Boomi, Power Automate) No-Code (Power Apps, SharePoint) SaaS/PaaS (Oracle, ServiceNow, Salesforce)
Power BI — Scoping Note
Power BI is a reporting and analytics platform (no-code paradigm). Governance principles in this master apply; however, Power BI-specific controls (workspace governance, dataset certification, row-level security, report deployment pipelines) are being defined by Reshmi Chakraborty's data team in a separate child SDLC. Until that child SDLC is published, Power BI changes require: ADO change record, peer review, and Technology Manager approval.

Applies to all roles involved in delivery: developers, integrators, architects, QA engineers, business analysts, product owners, and technology managers.

Infrastructure — Coordinating Separately
Cloud infrastructure provisioning (Terraform, Bicep, ARM templates, CloudFormation) is managed by Adam Houdashell's infrastructure team and is being addressed under a separate workstream. This SDLC governs application delivery. Infrastructure SDLC alignment will be incorporated in a future revision once that team's practices are reviewed.

2.3 Core Principles

PrincipleApplication

KISS (Keep It Simple)Simplest working solution is preferred; complexity must be justified
DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself)Single source of truth for logic, configuration, and data definitions
YAGNI (You Aren't Gonna Need It)Build what is required now; avoid speculative features and premature abstractions
Security by DesignSecurity is integrated at every phase — not bolted on at the end

Governance Architecture

3.1 Master–Child Model

The ABM Universal SDLC operates as a master document from which every team creates a child SDLC. The relationship is strictly additive: child documents extend the master for their specific platform, stack, or delivery context. No child document may remove, weaken, or override a control defined in this master.

3.2 Gatekeeper Responsibilities

The Chief Architect, under guidance of the Executive Sponsor, owns this document and is responsible for ensuring ABM SDLC standards are employed across all application teams.

  • Own and maintain this master document; manage version control via Git
  • Review and approve all child SDLC documents before publication
  • Periodically conduct child SDLC reviews to ensure ABM standards are being followed
  • Where gaps are identified in individual child documents, work with the application owner to remediate and bring the child SDLC into alignment
  • Evaluate and document exception requests from teams for any control variation

3.3 Git-Based Document Governance

This master and all child SDLC documents are version-controlled in Git. The workflow:

  • Repository: abm-it/sdlc-standards (Azure DevOps)
  • Master branch: Protected — direct commits prohibited
  • Updates to master: PR required → reviewed by Executive Sponsor or delegate → merged by gatekeeper
  • Child documents: Separate branches or subdirectories, PRs reviewed by gatekeeper before merge
  • Version tagging: Semantic versioning (1.0, 1.1, 2.0) on every approved release

Requirements & Planning

ISO A.8.26All Paradigms
Non-Negotiable Control: No development work begins until requirements have been reviewed with the Security team and a security green light has been obtained. This applies across all paradigms.

Development cannot begin without a documented, formally accepted requirement. The type of artifact depends on the paradigm:

All development work must identify and document non-functional requirements alongside functional requirements. NFRs are part of the FRD and are subject to the same security review gate. They drive architecture decisions, test plans, and release criteria.

Technical Design Document (TDD)

Once the FRD is accepted, the team produces a Technical Design Document (TDD) before any code is written. The TDD must address:

  • Integration architecture — API, file-based, messaging, or hybrid approach
  • Authentication and authorisation controls
  • Exception handling strategy and expected SLAs for failure scenarios
  • Data flow and data classification (PII, financial, operational)
  • Encryption requirements (at rest and in transit)
  • DRY validation — confirm no existing component or service is being duplicated

Security Review Gate

Before development begins, the team must walk Security through:

  • What is being built and why
  • Authentication and authorisation design
  • Data classification and any PII / financial data in scope
  • Encryption and data handling controls
  • Identified risks and proposed mitigations
Security sign-off is required before development starts. If a requirement or design does not meet security standards, a formal exception must be raised and approved before proceeding.
Security Exception Process
  1. Document the request: Technology Manager submits an exception via ADO/ServiceNow — control being waived, business justification, compensating controls, and proposed duration
  2. Security review: InfoSec team reviews and either approves (with conditions), requests modifications, or rejects
  3. Architecture sign-off: Chief Architect reviews technical risk and compensating controls
  4. Executive approval: Any zero-tolerance control exception requires Executive Sponsor approval
  5. Time-bound: Exceptions are valid for a maximum of 6 months. At expiry, the team must remediate or formally renew with escalated approval
  6. Exception register: All approved exceptions are logged in the register maintained by the Chief Architect and reviewed quarterly

Definition of Ready

A work item is ready for development when all three parties have given a green light:

  • Business owner — confirms the FRD accurately represents the requirement
  • Development lead / Architect — confirms technical approach is sound and TDD is complete
  • Security — confirms security review is complete and no unmitigated risks remain

Iterative Delivery

Teams keep work items small and shippable. Balance feature delivery with platform health (tech debt, security updates, dependency patching). Delivery model (sprint, kanban, or hybrid) is the team's choice — the gates above apply regardless.

Development Standards

ISO A.8.28Full-CodeLow-Code
All third-party and vendor development work must be executed under an ABM-approved vendor relationship with a valid Master Service Agreement (MSA). No vendor may access ABM systems, repositories, or data without an active MSA on file.

7.1 Universal Coding Standards

Apply to all programming languages and platforms. Team-specific additions are defined in child SDLC documents.

SOLID Principles (Mandatory for all object-oriented code)
PrincipleRule
S — Single ResponsibilityEach class/module has one reason to change; one clear purpose
O — Open/ClosedOpen for extension via interfaces; closed to direct modification
L — Liskov SubstitutionDerived types must be substitutable for their base types without altering behavior
I — Interface SegregationClients must not depend on methods they don't use; prefer focused interfaces
D — Dependency InversionDepend on abstractions; use dependency injection for all service dependencies

7.2 Code Quality Thresholds

The following metrics are enforced by CI/CD pipeline gates. PRs failing these thresholds are automatically blocked from merging. These objective, tool-measured thresholds provide auditable evidence that code quality standards are being met — they are not optional guidelines.

7.3 Definition of Done

A work item is only complete when ALL of the following are true:

  • Code complete and checked into feature branch following branching strategy
  • Unit tests written with ≥70% coverage; 100% of tests pass
  • Code complexity within thresholds (Coverlet / equivalent report attached to PR)
  • Code reviewed and approved by minimum 2 peers + Technology Manager
  • DRY validation — no code duplication introduced
  • SAST scan passed (zero High/Critical findings)
  • Merged to development branch and deployed to QA / equivalent environment
  • QA testing passed (QA exit criteria met)
  • Technical documentation / Wiki updated if applicable
  • Demo completed (P1 features require stakeholder demo)

7.4 AI-Assisted Development

AI tools may be used to accelerate development. AI-generated output is subject to the same review gates as human-authored code — the generating tool does not change the review obligation.

  • AI output is a draft — engineer review and accountability is mandatory before any AI-generated code is committed
  • Approved tools: Claude Code (code generation and review), CodeRabbit (automated PR review), Microsoft Copilot (documentation and code completion). Additional tools require Chief Architect approval before use.
  • No secrets, PII, or internal ABM data submitted to AI tools
  • AI-generated code must pass all quality gates identically to human-authored code
ISO A.8.28 Evidence
These coding standards, combined with PR review records showing security findings resolution and SAST reports from pipeline runs, constitute direct evidence for ISO 27001 A.8.28 (Secure coding).

Data Security & Integration Standards

ISO A.8.11ISO A.8.26All Paradigms
Non-Negotiable Control: All data in transit between ABM systems must be encrypted using TLS 1.2 or higher. All data at rest containing PII, financial records, or employee data must be encrypted. Any integration transmitting PII must be classified, documented, and security-reviewed before go-live.

8.1 Data Classification

Every data element handled by an ABM system must be classified. Classification drives encryption requirements, access controls, masking obligations, and retention policy. It is documented in the FRD data section and confirmed at the Security Review Gate.

InternalWork orders, operational records, application logs, non-sensitive configuration data~ Where practical✔ Mandatory~ Recommended
PublicPublished content, public-facing documentation, marketing materials— Not required~ Best practice— Not required

8.2 Encryption Standards

RequirementStandardApplies To
Encryption in transitTLS 1.2 minimum (TLS 1.3 preferred)All API calls, web traffic, inter-service communication, file transfers
Encryption at restAES-256All databases, file stores, and blob storage containing Restricted or Confidential data
API authenticationOAuth 2.0 / Azure AD (Entra ID) tokensAll internal and external API integrations

8.3 Integration Architecture — Hub and Spoke

ABM IT uses a hub-and-spoke integration architecture. Boomi AtomSphere is the primary central integration hub. All system-to-system data flows should route through the integration layer rather than creating direct point-to-point connections between applications.

Integration Topology Principle
Point-to-point integrations that bypass the integration hub are strongly discouraged — they create undocumented data flows, duplicate logic, and make PII inventory management difficult. New integrations must use the existing Boomi hub or Azure Logic Apps. Any exception (direct connection bypassing the hub) requires Chief Architect approval via a documented ADR, and must be flagged during the FTA design review.

8.5 PII Identification & Handling

Before any integration or feature goes live, the team must complete a PII impact assessment as part of the FRD / TDD:

  • Identify all PII fields transmitted or stored (name, address, SSN, date of birth, employee ID, pay data, health information)
  • Document PII in the FRD data classification section and the data flow diagram
  • Confirm encryption in transit and at rest for all Restricted and Confidential data fields
  • Confirm masking or anonymisation in all non-production environments before testing begins
  • Obtain Security team sign-off on the PII handling approach before development starts
  • Register any external PII flows with the InfoSec team's data flow inventory
Any integration transmitting employee PII (name, compensation, personal records) or customer data to an external system requires explicit Security team approval before go-live, and must be registered in the data flow inventory maintained by the InfoSec team. This applies to new integrations and to material changes to existing ones.

9.3 Test Data Handling

Mandatory Control — ISO A.8.33
Production data MUST NOT be used in non-production environments unless it has been fully anonymized or masked. This applies to all paradigms including SaaS configuration and integration testing.
  • Non-production environments use synthetic, anonymized, or masked data only
  • ABM Active Directory / Entra ID user accounts may be used across environments (this is the only approved exception)
  • Any test data containing PII, financial records, or employee data must be masked before use in non-prod
  • Test data lifecycle: creation → use → deletion must be logged; periodic reviews confirm no residual production data in non-prod
  • Technology Managers are accountable for confirming test data handling within their team's environments

10.4 Secret & Credential Management

Zero secrets in code or config files. All secrets, API keys, connection strings, and certificates must be in Azure Key Vault or approved equivalent. Any detected secret triggers immediate rotation and root cause analysis — no exceptions.
  • Application-level secrets: Azure Key Vault (or equivalent per platform)
  • IaC secrets: Terraform Cloud / pipeline secret variables — never in .tf files
  • Integration credentials (Boomi): Process extensions and connection definitions only
  • Secret rotation: Automated where supported; minimum quarterly manual rotation
  • Rotation log: SNOW ticket or ADO work item required for each rotation event

10.5 Threat Modeling

STRIDE threat modeling is required for all new services, data stores, and external integrations. For incremental feature additions, a lightweight security impact assessment is sufficient. Threat models are stored in SharePoint and referenced in the FTA.

11.3 Blue-Green Deployment (Production)

All production deployments use a blue-green strategy to achieve zero downtime and instant rollback capability:

  1. Deploy new version to inactive (green) environment slot
  2. Run automated smoke tests against green slot
  3. Monitor performance and error metrics for defined stabilization period
  4. Switch traffic to green on QA production certification
  5. Retain blue slot for 24-hour rollback window

11.4 Rollback Procedures

  • Authority: Technology Manager or on-call engineer
  • Trigger: P1 defect, data integrity issue, or security event post-deployment
  • Method: Revert to blue environment (blue-green) or re-run previous pipeline release
  • Communication: Immediate stakeholder notification via established channel
  • Documentation: Rollback SNOW ticket + post-incident RCA within 24 hours

12.4 Outsourced Development Controls

ISO A.8.30

When any ABM system component is developed or maintained by a third party:

  • Contract must include security clauses requiring OWASP compliance, vulnerability disclosure, and SDLC adherence
  • Vendor must provide current SOC 2 Type 2 report or ISO 27001 certification annually
  • Any ABM customizations or extensions to third-party platforms must follow this SDLC (CAB, SAST, environment separation)
  • Code review of vendor-delivered code is required before production acceptance
  • Vendor access to ABM systems is governed by least-privilege and time-bound access policies

Access Control & Segregation of Duties

ISO A.8.4ISO A.8.26All Paradigms

14.1 Core Principle

The person who builds a change is NEVER the person who approves it for production. Segregation of duties between developer, reviewer, and promoter is non-negotiable across all development paradigms.

14.2 Access Request Process

  1. Submit access request via ADO / ServiceNow with business justification
  2. Technology Manager reviews and approves (or rejects with documented reason)
  3. Access provisioned at minimum required permission level (least privilege)
  4. Monthly access reviews by Technology Manager for all environments
  5. Quarterly access reviews reported to the Executive Sponsor for audit evidence
  6. Immediate revocation on role change, team transfer, or departure
Access Complexity Varies by Platform
The baseline role/environment matrix below applies to full-code and integration platforms. SaaS platforms such as Oracle (HCM, ERP) can have significantly more complex access topologies — Oracle environments may span multiple development instances, sandboxes, and pilot configurations, each with their own role hierarchy. Oracle-specific access controls are addressed in the Oracle SaaS/PaaS child SDLC (Landry Baggett / Anjeli Daswani). Until that child SDLC is published, Oracle access provisioning requires Technology Manager approval for all environments and should be reviewed with the InfoSec team.

Child SDLC Extension Model

17.1 How to Create a Child SDLC

  1. Fork or branch abm-it/sdlc-standards under your team's directory
  2. Start from the Child SDLC Template (templates/child-sdlc-attestation.html)
  3. For each section of this master: mark as "Adopted as-is", "Extended — [describe additions]", or "Not Applicable — [justified reason]"
  4. Add team-specific sections (technology stack, tools, ceremonies, naming conventions)
  5. Submit PR to the sdlc-standards repo for gatekeeper review
  6. Gatekeeper approves → document published as the team's official child SDLC

17.2 What Child SDLCs May Add

  • Technology-specific tooling (e.g., Coverlet for .NET, test mode for Boomi)
  • Platform-specific release processes (e.g., MDM for mobile app deployment)
  • Additional ceremonies or artifacts relevant to the team
  • Stricter thresholds than the master (e.g., ≥80% test coverage instead of ≥70%)
  • Team-specific branching conventions within the universal model

17.3 What Child SDLCs May NOT Do

  • Remove, weaken, or override any control defined in this master
  • Lower thresholds below master minimums
  • Exempt any paradigm from the zero-tolerance controls
  • Skip the FTA, CAB/RFC, or peer review requirements
